## **Refactoring Within and Across Cycles**

  

Refactoring is structural modification without intentional semantic mutation.

  

It alters placement, containment, ordering, or constraint while preserving declared intent.

  

Rewriting changes substance.

  

Refactoring changes structure.

  

Within lifecycle discipline, refactoring occurs both within cycles and across cycles.

  

Cycles define dominant contraction objectives.

  

Refactoring defines structural adjustment within those objectives.

  

Drift risk increases when adjustment silently alters meaning.

  

Preventing drift requires explicit objective awareness tied to the governing cycle.

---

### **Refactoring Within a Cycle**

  

Within-cycle refactoring serves the dominant contraction objective of that lifecycle cycle.

  

Examples:

  

During structural stabilization, refactoring reorganizes sections without redefining claims.

  

During density refinement, refactoring compresses language without altering scope.

  

During audit enforcement, refactoring normalizes terminology without modifying architecture.

  

Within-cycle refactoring must remain subordinate to the declared contraction objective of the governing cycle.

  

If structural changes exceed that objective, lifecycle boundaries blur.

  

Boundary blurring increases variance.

  

Clear objective containment reduces destabilization risk.

---

### **Refactoring Across Cycles**

  

Refactoring may also occur across lifecycle cycles.

  

Cross-cycle refactoring must reinforce prior stabilization rather than reopen it.

  

Examples:

  

• Density refinement may expose architectural imbalance requiring reinforcement.

• Audit enforcement may reveal residual boundary ambiguity requiring clarification.

• Structural stabilization may require minor tightening discovered later.

  

These adjustments strengthen earlier decisions.

  

They should not reintroduce exploratory instability without explicit declaration.

  

Cross-cycle refactoring functions as reinforcement rather than regression when governed properly.

---

### **Drift Risk During Refactoring**

  

Refactoring modifies relationships.

  

Relationship changes are subtle.

  

Subtle change is harder to detect than overt rewriting.

  

Drift commonly emerges when:

  

• A section is relocated without re-evaluating its responsibility scope

• Compression removes boundary conditions

• Terminology normalization shifts semantic weight

• Layer adjustments blur abstraction levels

  

Drift tends to accumulate gradually.

  

It rarely appears as immediate contradiction.

  

Therefore refactoring must remain explicitly tied to the declared contraction objective of the governing cycle.

---

### **Safeguards Against Drift**

  

Drift prevention requires:

  

• Clear declaration of dominant contraction objective

• Preservation of defined scope and responsibilities

• Stability of terminology

• Separation between architectural change and stylistic change

• Verification during the audit cycle

  

When refactoring is constrained by these safeguards, convergence compounds.

  

When refactoring is unconstrained, structural destabilization risk increases.

---

### **Relationship to Lifecycle Discipline**

  

Lifecycle cycles define when certain types of refactoring are dominant:

  

Structural stabilization cycles emphasize structural refactoring.

  

Refinement cycles emphasize density-level refactoring.

  

Audit cycles emphasize governance-alignment refactoring.

  

Earlier cycles establish evaluable structured content.

  

Later cycles refine and verify it.

  

Refactoring may occur within and across cycles, but it must not reopen stabilized domains without explicit declaration.

  

Objective isolation supports structural integrity.

---

### **What This Enables**

  

When refactoring is disciplined within and across lifecycle cycles:

  

• Structural movement does not mutate meaning

• Density increases without altering scope

• Governance enforcement does not destabilize architecture

• Reinforcement strengthens rather than reopens prior decisions

• Convergence becomes more stable rather than fragmenting

  

Refactoring becomes controlled structural adjustment rather than uncontrolled mutation.

  

Controlled adjustment under lifecycle discipline supports durable scale within I-You-We-It.
